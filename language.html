Language

Sounds, writing, and structure.

Link to the Translator: https://lingojam.com/naunohhloh

  • Words are very generalized, so if you can't find a word, try using a synonym.
  • Words also typically only have the root form, so remove any "-ing", "-ed", "-ly", contractions, or other suffix.
  • Apostrophes are not recognized, use the expanded versions of words.
  • Articles (the, an, a) are not used.
Language Structure

  • This is an SOV language, meaning the word-order is Subject-Object-Verb, like in Japanese and Hindi. Like Spanish, adjectives come after nouns. "The brown cat ate a mouse" → "The cat brown a mouse ate"
  • Articles (the, an, a) are not used.
  • Past and present tense are communicated at the start of a sentence. "I will eat an apple" → "Future I am apple eat(ing)"
  • Possession: "Your nose" → "Nose of you"
  • Plurals are represented by repeating a word, except for when there is a specific number (Reduplication). "I see cats" → "I cat cat see", "I see four cats" → "I cat four see"
  • Numbers are spoken starting from the lowest value due to the writing system. "Four hundred and nine" → "Nine zero four"

Slang

"Life death" (Desihmihsed)

🠊

The circle of life, from the words life (desihm) and death (mihsed), which are palindromes. Can also be used to mean "it is what it is."

Nohmih, nohohmih, and nohohohmih

🠊

The word for happines, Nohmih, is sometines said with additional "oh" sounds when the speaker is exceptionally happy. In a playful setting, the amount of "oh"'s may go on for a comically long time.

Duhd noh N°ohnohmih

🠊

Roughly translates to "Have good, do good", or "Have happiness, give happiness". A life motto that's used to keep a positive attitude and encourage sharing and mutual aid.

"You are loved like a fish" or "fish (derogatory)"

🠊

Because a large part of displaying affection in the culture is through hair, and fish cannot grow hair or have it applied, this saying translates to "nobody loves you."

Numbers

Each section of the base form represents a larger digit (ones, tens, hundreds, etc.).

Because numbers are not used often and the culture is innately slow, it doesn't matter that they take a bit of time to decode.

Numbers are spoken from smallest digit to largest due to the left to right writing system.

Like time, they round to the nearest significant figure (one).

Idau/hl/

The Oele'in language, Idau/hl/ is both written and spoken, but much less casual. Due to the environment they live in, conversations take place over long distances and time periods.

Speech:
Oele'in use whistles and higher pitched noises to communicate between thick foliage. Due to time shortage, I created this language with the same words and sounds as Naunoh/hl/oh (with the addition of separate words for yellow and orange).

Writing:
The language is set up in a 3x4 grid structure, with the length of the vertical segments depending on how much space they have to write. Each mark takes up 1-2 boxes and is either vertical or horizontal.

Oele'in will peel the bark off of trees to access the softer trunk underneath where they can carve lettering in with their claws. Writing is typically done to warn others or give information on specific vegetation and creatures in the surrounding area. A thin strip of bark will be peeled off around the full circumference of the trunk as an indicator that there is writing if it may not be visible from all angles.

To mark the end of a word, the back of the thumb claw is pushed into the bark and pulled horizontally to create a soft indent. This is because due to the difficulty of peeling bark, space is used efficiently, and words often span over multiple vertical planes.
